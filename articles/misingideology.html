The Search for The Missing Ideology

April 24, 2025

Oscar Ward-Dutton

April 24, 2025 Oscar Ward-Dutton

WALKING on my daily commute in East London, I see communist posters littering the streets, about everything from culture under capitalism to labour strikes and LGBTQ+ rights. While I have some sympathy for them and smile at the campaigners handing out socialist newspapers outside the hospital, I can't help but think this is a sign of something bigger than a "leftist resurgence".

The problem is, trying to convince people of other economic systems other than capitalism while living under it is almost like trying to convince fish of a life outside of the ocean. Our children are often taught that humans are either inherently selfish or "irrational", and that the profit motive is the only way to convince people to do good things.

Every piece of media and education a Western child is exposed to is produced under capitalism, which is neither surprising nor particularly upsetting – after all, most of the past is a long line of people who knew nothing different. This begs the question, where in the world really deviates from the capitalist status quo? There may be regimes like North Korea or China which claim to be anti-capitalist, but are really just authoritarian dictatorships or Techno-Feudalist states respectively.

Maybe some countries are achieving growth and improving living standards, but simultaneously, first-world countries like the UK are seeing drastic wage stagnation, rising wealth inequality1 and a decline in living standards and life satisfaction2. This leads me to believe that modern capitalism, even when mediated by a government that claims to be both fiscally and socially progressive, is not going too well.

Challenging capitalism, however, is a difficult task, especially with its somewhat global economic consensus and the last three hundred years of true progress under its shadow. In many eyes, to challenge capitalism is to endorse communism – an absurd statement, but a popular belief nonetheless.

Although capitalism as an established idea is only about 300 years old, people can't really formulate an idea of what life was like before its hegemony – apart from the fact that it must have been terrible. The truth is, complex societies have existed since crop irrigation over 8,000 years ago, and we've been trying to find ways of governing these societies ever since. Neoliberal capitalism is just the latest in a series of experiments with this abstract idea of an economy.

If you're a political philosophy nerd like me, you might be thinking about the "Third Way". The Third Way is an idea whereby neoliberal economic theory is included for its promise of perfect supply and demand and infinite economic growth, but social democratic welfare is provided to try and mitigate its harmful effects. Almost like giving a raging bull a speedball and then employing an unpaid intern to run around the china shop and inject it with ketamine to calm it down in some kind of sadistic rodeo.

Neoliberalism rests on the belief that prioritising economic growth above all else will ultimately benefit everyone – by cutting taxes for the top 1%, their extra wealth is supposed to "trickle down" to the rest of society through higher tax revenues, increased spending, and new investments that fuel further expansion.

This is total and utter bullshit. Regardless of where you look, whenever these policies have been employed, wealth inequality has increased. When the rich get more wealth, they start to accrue passive income from their property – house prices go up, and when you own 10 houses3 it begins to add up. As the aim of capitalism is to accrue wealth, the rich then spend as little of that money as possible, and their assets continue to increase in value at your expense, providing them with an income with no labour. This process just keeps ordinary people below the elites in the economic food chain.

It doesn't matter how much money the government tries to throw at the problem through welfare: the poor are employed by the rich, and as soon as the money from their labour gets to the rich, it simply stays there.

This is why Liz Truss only got 44 days. The deregulation and tax cuts not only spooked the markets and crashed the pound, but they also would have had devastating effects on the working and middle classes if introduced.

So, isn't it about time we find something different? I'm really tired of people trying to convince others that all they need to do is work a little bit harder to be able to survive – but I'm also cross at the naivety of Marxists in believing that a centrally planned economy will ever be able to address the needs of the people in a considerate way.

If we want to create something new, we have to look at how we've survived for so long. The human race's greatest asset is our mental flexibility, which is currently being held back by unwieldy and oversized institutions hell-bent on upholding a harmful status quo long past its sell-by date. If we want to continue to survive, we must prove our agility and our ability to change.

The point is, we're not going to improve anything if we keep waiting for things to get better. We won't be saved by growth or productivity as long as they benefit the elites above all else.

If we want to change our system, we have to challenge everything.
